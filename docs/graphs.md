# Search Evaluation Charts

All charts are generated by running:

```bash
python scripts/generate_search_metrics.py
python scripts/plot_search_metrics.py
```

Output PNGs are written to `metrics/`. Each chart compares **raw search** (single category keyword, e.g. `"food"`) against **expanded search** (all synonym keywords for that category, e.g. `["food", "pizza", "taco", "sushi", ...]` derived from `backend/db.js`).

---

## chart_precision_recall.png

**What it shows:** Precision@50 and Recall@50 side by side for every category.

- **Precision@50** — of the top 50 results returned, what fraction are actually relevant? High precision means fewer irrelevant results.
- **Recall@50** — of all relevant events in the dataset, what fraction appear in the top 50? High recall means fewer relevant events are missed.

**What to look for:** Expanded search typically raises recall (more synonyms = more hits) at the cost of some precision (broader terms can pull in noise). A category where both bars are equal means the raw keyword alone is sufficient.

---

## chart_f1.png

**What it shows:** F1 score per category for raw vs expanded search.

F1 is the harmonic mean of precision and recall: `2 × P × R / (P + R)`. It collapses two numbers into one, penalizing approaches that sacrifice one metric to inflate the other.

**What to look for:** A higher F1 for expanded search confirms the synonym expansion is genuinely improving overall quality, not just trading precision for recall. Categories where raw F1 ≥ expanded F1 are candidates for trimming their keyword lists.

---

## chart_pak.png

**What it shows:** Mean Precision@K averaged across all categories, at K = 1, 3, 5, and 10.

Precision@K measures how many of the top K results are relevant — regardless of how many relevant events exist in total. It reflects the user's experience at the top of the result list, where attention is highest.

**What to look for:** If expanded search has higher P@1 and P@3, users see better results immediately. If raw search matches or beats expanded at low K but loses at K=10, the expansion is helping depth but not the very top results.

---

## chart_pr_curve.png

**What it shows:** Mean Precision–Recall curve using the standard 11-point interpolation, averaged across all categories that have at least one ground-truth event.

The curve is built by varying the result cutoff from 1 to 50 and plotting the (recall, precision) pair at each depth. A curve that stays higher and to the right is strictly better — it achieves higher precision at every recall level.

**What to look for:** The area under the curve (AUC) summarizes overall ranking quality. If the expanded curve dominates, keyword expansion improves ranking end-to-end. Curves that cross indicate a tradeoff: one method ranks the very top better while the other finds more relevant events deeper in the list.

---

## chart_score_dist.png

**What it shows:** Histograms of relevance scores assigned to events across all categories combined, one panel for raw search and one for expanded search.

Each event is scored by summing field-weighted term matches (title ×4, group ×3, description ×2, location/audience ×1). Events with score 0 are excluded.

**What to look for:** Expanded search should produce a broader, higher-score distribution — more events receive non-zero scores and at higher values. A distribution skewed toward score 1–2 for raw search but 3–8 for expanded confirms the extra keywords are matching more fields per event. A very flat, low distribution for expanded search would indicate the extra keywords are too generic.

---

## chart_gt_coverage.png

**What it shows:** The number of ground-truth relevant events per category in the current `scraped/events.json` dataset.

Ground truth for a category is defined as any event whose title or description contains at least one of that category's keywords.

**What to look for:** Categories with very few ground-truth events (1–2) make precision and recall unreliable — a single result swap flips the metric dramatically. Categories with many ground-truth events give more stable, meaningful scores. Use this chart to contextualize the other results; do not over-index on precision/recall numbers for thin categories.
